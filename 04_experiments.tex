\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We designed the following metrics to evaluate both teacher and student policy performance.
Let $\tau\in\{1,\dots,N\}$ index evaluation episodes and $t\in\{0,\dots,T_\tau\}$ index timesteps.
Unless otherwise stated, we run the evaluation episodes with a horizon of 10\,s.

\paragraph{Lift success (ever-lifted with hold).}
Let $h_t^\tau$ be the object height above the table and $H$ a lift threshold. An episode is considered
successful if the object remains above $H$ for at least $N_{\mathrm{hold}}$ consecutive steps. We define
$\mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\mathrm{lift}}
=\mathbb{I}\!\left[\exists\, t:\ \forall k\in\{0,\dots,N_{\mathrm{hold}}-1\},\ h_{t+k}^{\tau}\ge H\right]$
and report:
$\mathrm{LiftSucc}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{\tau=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\mathrm{lift}}.$

\paragraph{Unsafe episode rate.}
As stated above, an episode may terminate early due to one of the following conditions:
palm flipped ($\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{flip}}^{\tau}$), harmful collision ($\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{coll}}^{\tau}$),
object escape ($\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{obj}}^{\tau}$), or hand too far ($\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{hf}}^{\tau}$).
Let $\mathcal{M}=\{\mathrm{coll},\mathrm{obj},\mathrm{flip},\mathrm{hf}\}$ denote the set of termination modes,
and let $\mathbb{I}_m^\tau\in\{0,1\}$ indicate whether mode $m\in\mathcal{M}$ occurs in episode $\tau$.
An episode is unsafe if any termination mode is triggered:
$\mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}
=\mathbb{I}\!\left[\bigvee_{m\in\mathcal{M}} \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{m}=1\right]$. We report:
$\mathrm{UnsafeRateAvg}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{\tau=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}.$

\paragraph{Failure-mode decomposition.}
Each episode is assigned a single terminal mode $m^\tau\in\mathcal{M}$ from the
set of unsafe events (collision, object escape, palm flip, hand-too-far) as well
as timeout/success where applicable. We define
$\mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{m}=\mathbb{I}[m^\tau=m]$ and report
$\mathrm{FailureModeRate}(m)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{\tau=1}^{N}\mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{m}, m\in\mathcal{M}.$

When multiple unsafe conditions are triggered in the same timestep, we record
the first triggered termination condition (using a fixed priority order) as the
episode's failure mode.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}

The following ablation studies validate the design choices in our pipeline. We distill the teacher policies
trained during the RL phase into a compact, generalizable student policy. For RL training, we compare the
standard DexTrAH-RGB pipeline with our customized variant. For policy distillation, we evaluate five
methods---Vanilla DAgger, Vanilla SafeDAgger, \revision{fixed-threshold DexSafeDAgger, and our proposed
VLM-calibrated DexSafeDAgger, as well as a direct VLM-intervention variant}---in terms of lift-success growth, unsafe-episode rate, and intervention rate
throughout training. The intervention rate is defined as the fraction of timesteps at which the teacher takes
over, i.e., $\beta=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\mathbb{I}[g_t=1]$, averaged across evaluation episodes.
Because the unsafe-episode rate is our primary metric, we report both its average across the four ablation
objects and a per-object decomposition by failure mode, allowing us to assess the suitability of our pipeline
under different failure conditions. We define the ablation settings below.

% \noindent\textbf{(a) DexTrAH-RGB RL teacher + Vanilla DAgger.}
% We follow the DexTrAH-RGB teacher training setup and distill the resulting teacher into a student using
% Vanilla DAgger. Rollouts in simulation are fully controlled by the student policy, while the teacher runs
% in parallel in the background with the intervention rate $\beta$ fixed to $0$. At each timestep, the
% executed student action $a_t^{S}$ is relabeled with the corresponding teacher action $a_t^{T}$.

\noindent\textbf{(a) DexTrAH-RGB RL teacher + Vanilla DAgger.}
The teacher is trained using the DexTrAH-RGB RL recipe, and the student is learned via Vanilla DAgger.
This is the original DexTrAH-RGB pipeline used for the KUKA--Allegro setup.
During data collection, the student fully controls the rollout ($\beta=0$), while the teacher runs in parallel
to provide labels: each executed student action $a_t^{S}$ is relabeled with the corresponding teacher action
$a_t^{T}$.

% \noindent\textbf{(b) Our RL teacher + Vanilla DAgger.}
% We replace the DexTrAH-RGB teacher training pipeline with our customized RL recipe and use the same
% Vanilla DAgger-style online distillation.

\noindent\textbf{(b) Our RL teacher + Vanilla DAgger.}
This setting matches (a) except that the teacher is trained with our customized RL recipe instead of the
DexTrAH-RGB pipeline.

% \noindent\textbf{(c) Our RL teacher + Vanilla SafeDAgger.}
% The teacher policy is trained using our RL pipeline, and we extend Vanilla DAgger to a Vanilla SafeDAgger
% distillation setup. The student and teacher policies are rolled out in parallel, and the teacher action takes
% over only when the $\ell_2$ distance between the teacher and student actions exceeds a threshold, indicating
% a large teacher--student disagreement. The student is trained on teacher-labeled pairs $(o_t^{S}, a_t^{T})$,
% where $o_t^{S}$ denotes the student observation.

\noindent\textbf{(c) Our RL teacher + Vanilla SafeDAgger.}
Using the same teacher as in (b), we replace Vanilla DAgger with a disagreement-gated SafeDAgger baseline.
The teacher intervenes only when $\lVert a_t^{T}-a_t^{S}\rVert_2$ exceeds a threshold, and we train the student
on teacher-labeled pairs $(o_t^{S}, a_t^{T})$.

% \noindent\textbf{(d) DexTrAH-RGB RL teacher + SafeDAgger with disagreement and a risk predictor.}
% We follow the DexTrAH-RGB teacher training setup but apply our teacher-intervened distillation pipeline for
% student learning. The teacher and student policies are rolled out in parallel. Teacher intervention is triggered
% when either (i) the teacher disagrees with the student or (ii) the risk predictor flags the current transition
% as unlikely to lead to task success. The intervention decision is computed at every timestep. Likewise, the
% student is trained on teacher-labeled pairs $(o_t^{S}, a_t^{T})$.

\noindent\textbf{(d) DexTrAH-RGB RL teacher + SafeDAgger with disagreement and a risk predictor.}
This setting matches our full distillation pipeline but uses the DexTrAH-RGB-trained teacher (as in (a)).
Intervention is triggered by either large teacher--student disagreement or high predicted risk,
and the student is trained from teacher-labeled pairs $(o_t^{S}, a_t^{T})$ collected under this gate.

% \noindent\textbf{(e) Our RL teacher + SafeDAgger with disagreement and a risk predictor.}
% We combine our custom RL pipeline with the proposed teacher intervention mechanism, using both teacher--student
% disagreement and state-risk prediction to trigger teacher takeover.

\noindent\textbf{(e) Our RL teacher + SafeDAgger with disagreement and a risk predictor.}
\revision{This is the fixed-threshold version of our pipeline:} the teacher is trained with our customized RL recipe (as in (b)), and distillation
uses the same intervention gate as in (d), combining teacher--student disagreement and risk prediction
to trigger teacher takeover.

\noindent\revision{\textbf{(f) Our RL teacher + SafeDAgger with disagreement, a risk predictor, and VLM tuning.}
This setting extends (e) with the proposed VLM tuner, which periodically
calibrates the disagreement and risk thresholds using rollout
statistics and representative visual states.}

\noindent\revision{\textbf{(g) Our RL teacher + direct VLM intervention.}
To distinguish threshold calibration from direct VLM arbitration, this variant
removes the rule-based intervention gate in Eq.~\eqref{eq:adaptive_gate}. Instead
of serving as a high-level advisor that calibrates the disagreement and risk
thresholds, the VLM directly outputs the binary intervention decision
$g_t\in\{0,1\}$ and therefore determines whether the teacher or student action
is executed. The student is trained on the same teacher-labeled pairs as in the
other intervention-based variants.}

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:ablation_studies}, ablations (a) and (d), which use the DexTrAH-RGB RL recipe,
exhibit lower lift success and higher unsafe rate under both Vanilla DAgger and our intervention
mechanism. We attribute this gap to limited transferability of the DexTrAH-RGB teacher training setup: it
relies heavily on a \textsc{Fabrics} controller and was tuned for the KUKA--Allegro platform. When applied to
our hardware and task setting, the resulting teacher policies degrade due to mismatched dynamics and
nontrivial hyperparameter retuning. Consequently, the weaker teacher policy set constrains student
distillation performance in downstream training.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation}, ablations (b), (c), (e), and (f), which adopt our RL pipeline, reach
comparable final lift success on the four ablation objects (left). This is expected because our
intervention mechanism is designed primarily to suppress unsafe exploration rather than directly optimize
task success. Vanilla DAgger keeps the student in full control ($\beta=0$),
whereas the three intervention variants exhibit a decreasing intervention rate
as the student improves (middle). These methods substantially reduce unsafe
episodes (by approximately 80\% relative to Vanilla DAgger)
early in training and maintain a low unsafe rate (below 20\%) throughout distillation, with the proposed
method achieving the lowest unsafe rate overall (right).

Compared with Vanilla SafeDAgger, our method intervenes more frequently because the risk predictor
identifies additional high-risk transitions, leading to more teacher takeovers. This increased
supervision further reduces the unsafe rate (by about 5 percentage points on average). By the end of training, our method
converges toward the teacher policy's average unsafe rate ($\approx 12\%$) and reaches a minimum unsafe rate
($\approx 8\%$) (both averaged over the four ablation objects), which is below the teacher average.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:failure_modes}, the per-object failure-mode decomposition indicates that
unsafe episodes are dominated by harmful collisions and object escape across most objects. Relative
to vanilla DAgger, both intervention-based methods substantially reduce the per-object unsafe rate and
consistently suppress these two dominant failure modes. Moreover, disagreement-based SafeDAgger
already eliminates many of the large early spikes in collision and escape, suggesting that
teacher takeovers effectively prevent the student from entering highly unsafe regions of the state
space during the initial distillation phase. 

The combination of teacher--student disagreement with the risk predictor further improves safety,
yielding the lowest unsafe rate for most objects. This gain is primarily attributable to additional
reductions in collision- and escape-induced terminations, especially in cases where disagreement
alone is insufficient to anticipate downstream failures. Overall, the decomposition supports our
design choice: augmenting disagreement with a learned risk signal provides a stricter intervention
criterion that reduces unsafe exploration while maintaining competitive task performance.

\subsection{Real-World Experiments Setup}

The experiments exhibit a lower lift success rate and a higher unsafe episode rate than
the teacher and student evaluation results in simulation, indicating that the proposed pipeline does
not fully eliminate the sim-to-real gap. Nevertheless, the resulting policy is sufficiently robust
for real-world deployment.

The unseen-object evaluation reveals a clear generalization gap: compared to
seen objects (WindCart/Boot/ToolBox/Cow), the policy achieves lower lift success
and higher unsafe rates on unseen objects (Plane/Shoe). This degradation is
primarily driven by increased harmful collisions (20.0\% and 26.7\% on Plane and
Shoe), while object escape remains comparable across objects
($\approx$6.7--13.3\%). No palm-flip or hand-too-far events are observed,
suggesting that real-world failures are dominated by contact-rich interactions
rather than gross pose instabilities. Overall, the results indicate that the
remaining sim-to-real gap on unseen geometries is largely attributable to a
contact-strategy mismatch, i.e., the policy fails to adapt its contact
interactions to novel object shapes.

\subsection{Discussion}

While effectively reducing unsafe exploration, the teacher-intervened online distillation process
appears to reduce sample efficiency. In Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation}, we observe slower performance growth in
lift-success evaluation under higher intervention rates. The underlying cause remains unclear; we believe
there is room for improvement in both exploration safety and sample efficiency within the online
distillation paradigm.
