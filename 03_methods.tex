\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We consider online interactive imitation learning with teacher intervention in a
partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP)
$\mathcal{M}=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},\mathcal{O},P,\Omega,r,\gamma)$, with
privileged teacher state $s_t\in\mathcal{S}$, deployable student observation
$o_t\in\mathcal{O}$ (RGB), action $a_t\in\mathcal{A}$, observation model
$o_t\sim\Omega(\cdot\mid s_t)$, and dynamics
$s_{t+1}\sim P(\cdot\mid s_t,a_t)$. During training, we assume access to a set
of privileged teacher policies $\{\pi_{T_i}\}_{i=1}^{K}$, whereas at deployment
the student policy $\pi_S$ must act solely from $o_t$. Our goal is to distill
the collective teacher behavior into an RGB-based student that achieves high
task success while minimizing unsafe exploration during training.

The induced rollout distribution $p_{\pi_{\mathcal{G}}}$ is defined by the gated rollout policy
$\pi_{\mathcal{G}}$, which switches between the teacher and student policies
according to the intervention gate. Its learning objective and intervention
gate are
\begingroup
\small
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\min_{\substack{\pi_S\in\Pi_S\\ \mathcal{G}\in\mathfrak{G}}}
\;
&
\Bigg[
\underbrace{
-\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim p_{\pi_{\mathcal{G}}}}\!\left[\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{succ}}(\tau)\right]
}_{\text{maximize success}}
\;+\;
\lambda\,
\underbrace{
\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim p_{\pi_{\mathcal{G}}}}\!\left[
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}(s_t,a_t)
\right]}_{\text{minimize unsafe}}
\Bigg]
\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
&
a_t=g_t a_t^T+(1-g_t)a_t^S,\\
&
g_t=\mathcal{G}(s_t,o_t;\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(k)})\in\{0,1\},\\
&
s_{t+1}\sim P(\cdot\mid s_t,a_t),\qquad
o_t\sim\Omega(\cdot\mid s_t).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:vc_objective}
\end{equation}
\endgroup
where $g_t=1$ indicates teacher takeover and $g_t=0$ denotes student control.
The intervention policy is parameterized by $\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(k)}=
(\tau_\delta^{(k)},\tau_Q^{(k)})$, which governs teacher takeover decisions in $\mathcal{G}$. Periodically, a VLM proposes a displacement
$\Delta\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(k)}$, from which the policy is updated to adjust to distributional shift.

We formalize task success via a trajectory-level predicate
$\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{succ}}(\tau)\in\{0,1\}$, which is $1$ if the object is
grasped, lifted, and held above a height threshold for a consecutive duration.
Safety is governed by early termination conditions: letting
$\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}(s_t,a_t)\in\{0,1\}$ indicate whether any unsafe
event (e.g., harmful collision, object escape, palm flip, hand-too-far) occurs
at time $t$, the trajectory is safe if
$\prod_{t=0}^{T-1}\big(1-\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}(s_t)\big)=1$.

\subsection{Overview of the Distillation Pipeline}
Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline} summarizes \methodname, our teacher--student distillation pipeline,
which consists of five core components: (i) privileged teacher policy training
in simulation (Sec.~C), (ii) risk predictor learning to estimate lift success
under student actions (Sec.~D), (iii) deterministic teacher intervention
(Sec.~E), (iv) VLM-guided intervention policy adaptation (Sec.~F), and (v) online
student skill distillation (Sec.~G).

The privileged teacher is first trained in simulation, and its rollouts provide
an offline warm start for the Bellman success-value critic. The critic estimates
prospective lift success under the student action and continues to be updated
from a ring replay buffer.

During online distillation, the teacher intervenes when either teacher--student
action disagreement is high or the critic predicts low success. The student is
then optimized online from teacher labels collected under the resulting rollout
policy $\pi_{\mathcal{G}}$. A VLM is periodically queried to propose updates to
the intervention policy's operating point. The resulting candidate policy parameters
are clipped and smoothed before use. Pseudo-code is
provided in Alg.~\ref{alg:safe_dagger}.

\subsection{Privileged Teacher Policy Training}
Teacher policy learning is formulated as a reinforcement learning problem with
transition tuples $\langle s_t,a_t,r_t,s_{t+1}\rangle$, where $s_t$ is the
privileged state, $a_t$ is the action, $r_t$ is the reward, and $s_{t+1}$ is the
next state. The objective is to maximize the expected discounted return,
\begingroup
\small
\begin{equation}
\pi_T^*
=
\arg\max_{\pi_T}\;
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_T}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\gamma^t r_t
\right].
\label{eq:teacher_rl_objective}
\end{equation}
\endgroup
We use a shaped reward $r_t=\sum_{k=1}^{K_r} r_{t,k}$ to encourage stable grasp
acquisition, object lifting, and goal reaching (Table~\ref{tab:reward_terms}).

The teacher is trained in simulation using an asymmetric actor--critic setup.
Details of the actor and critic observation spaces are provided in the Appendix.
The actor receives noisy and partial proprioceptive observations that approximate
realistic sensing, while the critic is provided with the privileged full state
of both the robot and object, available only in simulation.

Episodes terminate at
\begingroup
\small
\begin{equation}
T = \min\left\{t \,\middle|\, \mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}(s_t)=1
\;\;\text{or}\;\; t \ge T_{\max}\right\},
\end{equation}
\endgroup
where $T_{\max}=10\,\mathrm{s}$ is the timeout horizon. Early termination
conditions include palm flip, harmful collision, object escape, and hand-too-far
(see Sec.~A), and are treated as unsafe events for both teacher training and
subsequent intervention decisions.

\subsection{Risk Predictor}
$\ell_2$ action disagreement alone is insufficient as an intervention signal and may miss small
action deviations that could cause unstable grasps, collisions, or object escape.
We therefore learn a success-value critic, trained with lift-success labels, that estimates the
probability of safe lift success for the current student state--action pair and provides a
prospective signal complementary to action disagreement.

The predictor evaluates a multimodal state--action pair $(s_t,a_t^S)$, where
$s_t$ combines robot proprioception and embeddings from a visual encoder. The
encoder is trained end-to-end during skill distillation and regularized by the
auxiliary object-position prediction loss in Sec.~G. The twin critics
$Q_1$ and $Q_2$ are ReLU MLPs with hidden dimensions $[256,128]$. Each critic's
logit $z_i(s,a)$ is mapped to a success probability by
$Q_i(s,a)=\sigma(z_i(s,a)/T)$, with calibration temperature $T=4$.
We use the conservative estimate
$\hat Q_{\mathrm{succ}}(s,a)=\min_{i\in\{1,2\}}Q_i(s,a)$; lower values indicate
higher prospective risk.

The critics are pretrained on teacher transitions and updated online from a ring
replay buffer. The label $u_t\in\{0,1\}$ indicates safe lift success: it is $1$
only when the lift criterion is satisfied without an unsafe event. Training
uses the SARSA-style target
\begingroup
\small
\begin{equation}
y_t=
\operatorname{clip}\!\left(
u_t+\gamma(1-d_t^{\mathrm{eff}})
\min_{i\in\{1,2\}}Q_i^{\mathrm{targ}}(s_{t+1},a_{t+1}),
\,0,\,1\right),
\label{eq:success_target}
\end{equation}
\endgroup
where $d_t$ is the termination flag, $h_t$ indicates whether $a_{t+1}$ is valid,
$d_t^{\mathrm{eff}}=\max(d_t,1-h_t)$, and $Q_i^{\mathrm{targ}}$ are slowly
updated target networks. Both critics minimize the mean-squared error (MSE)
between their predictions and $y_t$; the minimum across critics reduces overestimation.

\subsection{Intervention Policy}
We use a deterministic two-channel safety filter for teacher arbitration. The
first channel measures teacher--student disagreement,
$\delta_t\coloneqq\lVert a_t^S-a_t^T\rVert_2$, where
$a_t^T\sim\pi_T(\cdot\mid s_t)$ and $a_t^S\sim\pi_S(\cdot\mid o_t)$. The second
channel evaluates prospective task safety through
$\hat Q_{\mathrm{succ}}(s_t,a_t^S)$. The intervention policy is parameterized by
$\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(k)}=(\tau_\delta^{(k)},\tau_Q^{(k)})$, where
$\tau_\delta^{(k)}$ is the maximum admissible disagreement and
$\tau_Q^{(k)}$ is the minimum acceptable predicted success.

These parameters define the deterministic intervention gate:
\begingroup
\small
\begin{equation}
g_t
=\mathbb{I}\!\left[
\hat Q_{\mathrm{succ}}(s_t,a_t^S)<\tau_Q^{(k)}
\;\lor\;\delta_t>\tau_\delta^{(k)}
\right].
\label{eq:adaptive_gate}
\end{equation}
\endgroup
Thus, either large disagreement or low predicted success triggers teacher
takeover. Decreasing $\tau_\delta$ or increasing $\tau_Q$ makes the intervention
policy more conservative; the opposite shift increases student autonomy.

\revision{We initialize $\tau_\delta^{(0)}$ and $\tau_Q^{(0)}$ from the $90$th
percentile of warm-start disagreement and the $10$th percentile of predicted
success over successful teacher rollouts, respectively. These values define the
initial operating point $\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(0)}$.}

\subsection{VLM-Guided Intervention Policy Adaptation}
\label{subsec:vlm_threshold_tuner}
\begingroup
\color{yellow!50!black}
During online distillation, the student policy, visited-state distribution, and risk-predictor calibration co-evolve, and the dominant failure modes may also change. A stationary intervention mechanism therefore cannot maintain a consistent safety--autonomy trade-off. We periodically query a VLM to guide the displacement of intervention parameters.
At iteration $k$, the VLM receives rollout statistics $\mathcal{C}_k$,
including disagreement ($\delta_t$), predicted success
($\hat Q_{\mathrm{succ}}(s_t,a_t^S)$), intervention rate ($\beta$), and
unsafe-episode rate ($\mathrm{UnsafeRateAvg}$). It also receives a
representative image batch $\mathcal{V}_k$ sampled from the visual buffer
$\mathcal{B}_V$ to cover high-disagreement, low-success, and unsafe states.
From this context, it proposes the policy update
$\Delta\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(k)}=
(\Delta\tau_\delta^{(k)},\Delta\tau_Q^{(k)})$, producing the candidate
operating point
$\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}^{(k)}=
\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(k)}+\Delta\boldsymbol{\tau}^{(k)}$.

Each candidate policy parameter is clipped around its initial value and
exponentially smoothed:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\tau_{x}^{(k+1)}
&=(1-\alpha)\tau_{x}^{(k)}
+\alpha\,\operatorname{clip}\!\left(
\tilde{\tau}_{x}^{(k)},
c_{x}^{\min}\tau_{x}^{(0)},
c_{x}^{\max}\tau_{x}^{(0)}\right),\\[-2pt]
&\hspace{5.5em}x\in\{\delta,Q\}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:vlm_threshold_update}
\end{equation}
Here, $\alpha$ controls the adaptation rate, and $c_x^{\min}$ and
$c_x^{\max}$ define the admissible calibration region around
$\tau_x^{(0)}$.
\endgroup


\subsection{Student Skill Distillation}
Student learning is posed as supervised imitation from teacher-labeled
transitions collected under the gated rollout policy $\pi_{\mathcal{G}}$. The
student observes RGB images (two stereo views) together with robot proprioception.

We optimize a combined objective
$\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{action}}+\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}$, where the
auxiliary loss predicts object position to regularize the visual encoder,
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}=\lVert
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathrm{obj}}-\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{obj}}\rVert_2$.

For action imitation, we use a weighted $L_2$ loss,
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{action}}
=\lVert\mathbf{a}_S-\mathbf{a}_T\rVert_W^2
=(\mathbf{a}_S-\mathbf{a}_T)^\top W(\mathbf{a}_S-\mathbf{a}_T)$,
with $W=\mathrm{diag}(w_1,\dots,w_d)$. We set
$w_j=\sigma_{0,j}^{-2}$ using a reference scale $\sigma_{0,j}$ (e.g., the
teacher action standard deviation on dimension $j$), yielding
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{action}}=
\sum_{j=1}^{d}(a_{S,j}-a_{T,j})^2/\sigma_{0,j}^{2}$.

Training proceeds in two stages. (i) \emph{Warm-start:} we collect an offline
dataset (e.g., $100$k transitions) under full teacher control ($g_t\equiv 1$)
to pretrain the risk predictor. (ii) \emph{Online
distillation:} we perform interactive rollouts under $\pi_{\mathcal{G}}$ and
query teacher labels on intervened steps. The student is updated online using
the current-step teacher label only; no replay buffer or offline dataset is
maintained for student updating. The design reduces the
effect of stale supervision and stabilizes online imitation updates.
